# Audience Lenses

Every creative decision downstream of this skill should be traceable to one of these two lenses, or an explicit named blend. Pick one before recommending format, narrative, pacing, or style.

## Lens 1: Enterprise (Unilever-style)

**Who's actually in the room (or will review before it ships):**
- Brand/marketing team enforcing a locked visual identity
- Legal/compliance, especially for claims, data, or regulated markets
- Regional/local marketing teams who may need to re-skin or translate it
- Procurement or IT if it touches a real product/tool
- An executive audience with limited patience for anything that reads as "an ad"

**What this changes:**
- **Editability beats novelty.** If it's a deck, it must be editable by someone who isn't you. Prefer `presentation-motion` real-tool output over a locked HTML file unless the ask is explicitly a web-hosted teaser.
- **Multi-market from the start.** Assume translation/localization. Keep text short, avoid idioms, avoid baked-in text where a live text layer would survive translation better.
- **Claims need proof.** Every stat or claim on screen should be defensible; flag anything that looks like a claim a legal reviewer would question.
- **Risk tolerance is low, ambition doesn't have to be.** "Enterprise" is not a synonym for "boring" — Unilever-scale companies run genuinely excellent internal and external creative. It means: more review gates, more stakeholders, more need for restraint in claims and legal language, not necessarily restraint in craft.
- **Pacing:** longer holds are fine and often preferred — the room is controlled (town hall, QBR, all-hands), not a scroll-past feed. Silence and a clean beat land better than rapid-fire cuts.
- **Sound:** must have a clean silent-with-captions version. Assume some viewings are muted (open-plan office, town hall stream).
- **Distribution reality:** SharePoint/Teams embeds, internal portals, occasionally LinkedIn for external comms. Assume corporate network file-size and codec constraints.

**Good creative-director questions for this lens:**
- Who signs off on this before it ships — brand, legal, regional, all three?
- Does this need to work in a market outside the one it was written for?
- Is there a locked brand guideline or template we must start from? (If none exists yet, hand off to `brand-motion-system` → `extracting-from-existing-brand.md`, or note it needs to be requested.)
- What's the room this plays in — live town hall, async Teams share, external LinkedIn post?

## Lens 2: Startup / scale-up

**Who's actually in the room:**
- Usually one founder or a 2-3 person team making the call
- No dedicated brand/legal review (or a very light one)
- The audience is often cold — a scroller, a prospective user, an investor skimming a deck between meetings

**What this changes:**
- **Speed beats polish.** A sharp idea shipped this week beats a polished one shipped next month. Rougher production values are fine, even a feature, if they read as authentic rather than sloppy.
- **First 2 seconds are everything.** No slow enterprise-style open. Lead with the hook — the pain, the surprising result, the bold claim — immediately.
- **Founder voice is an asset.** First-person, direct, opinionated copy tends to outperform generic "solutions" language.
- **No design team assumption.** Default to `brand-motion-system` → `synthesizing-for-startups.md` if there's no visual identity yet, rather than waiting on one.
- **Pacing:** fast. Startups rarely get the benefit of a captive room — assume a scroll, assume skip-ahead, assume silent autoplay.
- **Length discipline:** shorter than the enterprise default almost every time. If in doubt, cut a scene rather than slow one down.
- **Distribution reality:** LinkedIn, X, a landing page hero, an investor update embed, a demo-day stage. Optimize for silent-first, mobile-first.

**Good creative-director questions for this lens:**
- Is this cold-audience acquisition content, or warm content for people already evaluating the product (investor, existing user)?
- Do we have any visual identity yet, or are we defining it as part of this?
- What's the platform it lives on first — that decides aspect ratio and the first-2-second rule.

## Hybrid cases

Common and legitimate — name it explicitly rather than forcing a single lens:
- An internal tool team inside a large enterprise that ships like a startup (small team, fast iteration) but still needs to clear brand/legal before wide internal release.
- A startup selling into enterprise buyers (B2B SaaS) — startup production speed, but the demo needs to answer enterprise buyer concerns (security, compliance, ROI) that a consumer-startup demo wouldn't need.

When hybrid, state which parts of the lens apply from each side, e.g.: *"Startup production speed and founder voice, but the demo needs an ROI/compliance beat because the buyer is an enterprise IT team."*
