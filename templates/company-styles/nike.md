# Style DNA: Nike

> **Disclaimer:** This describes stylistic and motion *patterns* commonly observed in Nike's public brand-film content, for creative-direction reference only. Never reproduce Nike's actual logo, athlete likenesses, footage, or copyrighted material. Adapt the pattern to your own brand — see `.claude/skills/brand-motion-system/references/reference-company-styles.md`.

## Color & type mood

- High contrast throughout — deep blacks and blown-out highlights rather than a mid-tone, comfortable palette.
- Bold, condensed, heavy-weight type used at large scale — type is treated as a graphic/kinetic element in its own right, not just a label.
- Color is often monochrome or near-monochrome with a single sharp accent (frequently drawn from a product's own color) rather than a soft brand palette.

## Motion signature

- Kinetic type: words build, slam, or cut into frame in rhythm with a beat — this register's signature move is type that moves like a percussion instrument, not a caption.
- Rhythm-driven cuts: edit pace is locked to a musical or physical rhythm (a runner's footfall, a heartbeat) rather than to a fixed narrative-beat timer — cuts land *on* the beat.
- High-energy, athletic/action-first visual language — motion should feel physical (impact, momentum, effort) rather than smooth and considered.
- Minimal holds — this register rarely lingers; energy is maintained by near-constant motion, which is a deliberate exception to this repo's general "60/40 hold/motion" default (see `references/html-video-teasers/scenes-and-pacing.md`) — Nike's register earns the exception through rhythm, not randomness.

## Sound signature

- Music-led, often the actual structuring device of the edit (cuts synced to the track) — a driving, physical, often percussive bed. SFX are physical/impact-based (a footstep thud, a snap) rather than digital/UI-style.

## Typical narrative arc

Hero's Journey (compressed), built around effort and payoff rather than product features — struggle/effort shown viscerally, a turning point (often the product appearing at the moment of achievement), triumphant close. See `.claude/skills/creative-director/references/narrative-structures.md`.

## Applied storyboard skeleton (generic brand film)

| Scene | Time | Purpose | Motion | Notes |
|---|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | 0-6s | Physical cold open, no logo | Rhythmic cuts locked to a beat | Energy from frame one |
| 2 | 6-20s | Effort/struggle montage | Kinetic type punctuates key moments | Cuts on the beat, not on a timer |
| 3 | 20-26s | Turning point | Product appears at the moment of achievement | The one true "hold" of the piece |
| 4 | 26-32s | Triumph | High-energy resolution | Type slams in with the final claim |
| 5 | 32-36s | Close | Brief, confident mark | No lingering CTA card |

## Best-fit audience

Consumer brand films, culture and recruiting content, anything where physical energy and emotional payoff matter more than explaining a feature. A poor fit for enterprise B2B or dev-tool content — the register's constant motion and lack of restraint reads as off-tone for a considered-purchase or technical audience.
